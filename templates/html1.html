First, let us begin by introducing the most important building block of the Web: HTML.


HTML, which stands for Hyper Text Markup Language is a markup language (not to be confused with a programming language), which is used to explain the structure of the page to the web browser. In order to distinguish between different types of content, different building blocks are used, and these building blocks are called ELEMENTS.


It is very important to understand that HTML is only responsible for the structure: it describes the structure SEMANTICALLY, not graphically or logically. It wasn't always the case, but such restriction is the only feasible solution to avoid intersections between HTML and other adjacent technologies.

{{getheader("Brief history")}}

HTML was created in 1989 by Tim Berners-Lee, when he was working at CERN, as a way to structure and standardize content on the internet. The first publicly available mention of HTML was in a document called "HTML tags". It describes the first 18 elements, comprising the initial, relatively simple design of HTML. Later, however, when the Internet became more and more popular, a conflict emerged: the creator of HTML was unsatisfied with the direction that commercial applications of HTML were taking it, and wanted a complete revamp (see XHTML for more), while commercial users preferred practical applications over rigor, chosing not to make any sharp turns and maintain backwards-compatibility and uniformity. They also established their own working group, known as WHATWG, which was resposible for development of HTML5. Although until recently, W3C was still maintaining the oficial standard.


The design principles of HTML is a complicated topic in general, but if you wish to learn more, you can do so here. For our current purposes, we will focus more on the practical side.
